CS 5004/5: Object-Oriented Design

Fall 2022

Course Description

This course presents a comparative approach to object-oriented programming and design including concepts such as objects, classes, metaclasses, messages, methods, inheritances, and generics. It is the next course in the Align "sequence" after CS 5001/3.

We will review polymorphism in object-oriented languages, contrast inheritance and composition as dual techniques for software reuse, and dive deeper into the principles of object-oriented programming and design such as software components, design patterns, and graphical design notations.

We will illustrate basic concepts in object-oriented design with case studies in application frameworks and by writing programs in Java.

 

Professor: Rasika Bhalerao

TAs:

  • Minyi (Amy) Xu (xu.miny@northeastern.edu)
  • Zhenzhou Zhou (zhou.zhenz@northeastern.edu)

Learning Goals

  1. Develop a basic understanding of object-oriented design using Java.
  2. Determine functionality of object-oriented code written by oneself and others through reading and tracing short segments of code.
  3. Write correct and clearly-documented object-oriented code that others can read, understand, and modify.
  4. Use polymorphism, inheritance, composition, and metaclasses to design and write efficient code.
  5. Develop unit tests to exercise implemented object-oriented code and test edge cases.

Course Structure

Each week there will be a lecture section (5004, on Thursdays) and a lab section (5005, on Mondays).

During the lecture section we will go over new content using lecture, live coding, discussion, and ungraded individual exercises. For each week's lecture content, there is:

  • A quiz (go to the Quizzes tab on the left).
  • A module which is a set of supplementary lecture notes (Modules tab on the left). There is no requirement involving them, but they can be helpful.

For each week's lab content, there is:

  • An in-class lab exercise which will cover the new coding concepts introduced that week (find it at the end of each Module).
  • A homework project which is a practical coding assignment (Assignments tab on the left).

There is also a midterm exam and a final exam.

 

OFFICE HOURS:

The Professor's office hours for this course will be one hour before lecture on Thursdays. Individual one-on-one meetings are also available by appointment.

Amy's office hours are Mondays and Tuesdays 9am - 10am PST at https://northeastern.zoom.us/j/6103597793 (Amy). ZhenZhou's office hours are Wednesdays and Fridays 10 - 11am PST on MS Teams.

 

All of the pieces are designed to work together to give you practice with the material and experience implementing more comprehensive coding projects to help you learn.

 

How to succeed in this course:

  • Ask questions during the lecture and lab sections. We expect you to ask questions and run into issues where you may need help.
  • Start the homework projects early. Come to lab each Monday having read the instructions for that week's project, and bring questions.
  • Use the regular course materials to get practice coding and to study for the midterm and final exams. The exams will just be a longer quiz that covers more material.

Grading

The goal of this course is to teach you the material to a certain level of mastery. Quizzes will provide you with rapid and frequent feedback about how well you understand the most recent topic. The labs will give you supervised time to learn new concepts, and the homework projects will give you the chance to design and write more complex and creative code.

Assessment will be based on quizzes, projects, and exams. You may ask for feedback on anything else, but it will not figure into your final grade.

Grade Breakdown
Homework Projects 30%
Lab Exercises 20%
Quizzes 20%
Midterm Exam 15%
Final Exam 15%

Quizzes will be graded and handed back with feedback for any problem you attempt. You will be able to retake a quiz as many times as you wish up to the deadline. We will drop your lowest quiz grade, so one silly mistake or a bad week won’t affect your overall grade.

Projects will be graded and handed back with feedback. All projects will be graded out of 30 points. Code functionality, code organization, and quality comments will constitute 26 points. A brief report or document with your results will constitute 4 points.

Final grades will be assigned based on the overall percentage calculated using the weightings listed above. There is no absolute direct mapping to letter grades, but the minimum overall percentage required to obtain each letter grade will be no higher than the following: A (93%), A- (90%), B+ (87%), B (83%), B- (80%), C- (65%).

To progress, students are required to meet the grade point average (GPA) requirements for the MS Computer Science -- Align as determined by Khoury College of Computer Sciences (see Khoury's website for more information).

Late Policy

Most weeks there will be a project and a quiz (due dates are in Canvas). You may retake the quiz until the deadline.

During the semester, you have eight no fault "time travel days" to hand in a project after a deadline without penalty for being late. You can distribute the eight time travel days among whichever projects you see fit, but you can use at most three days for any single project. The time travel days are considered integers (no partial days). In order to use a time travel day, you must notify the professor before the regular deadline.

Asking for Help

Computer Science is likely relatively new for you if you are taking this course. One of the goals for this course is to show you that CS can be fun. It can be challenging, it can be difficult, and it can be extremely satisfying when something works correctly. It should not be frustrating.

Frustration occurs when you are working on something and not making progress, especially if you’re not sure about what you are doing. Frustration does not facilitate learning. If you find yourself getting frustrated, stop. Go do something else, eat some dinner, take a walk, but most importantly, ask a question. Ask the professor or a TA, ask a question in lab or class, talk with a classmate, or set up a 1-1 session to go over your code. 

We do not expect you to complete the projects completely on your own.

We suggest the 30-minute rule: if you have been stuck on a problem for more than 30 minutes and have made no progress, despite your best efforts, please stop and get help. Ask the professor or a TA, or consult a peer. If you don't get an answer immediately, do something else for a while. Please do not waste your time on one problem or bug in your code. Asking a question can get you past the bug quickly and teach you how to fix it on your own next time.

Stuff Happens Clause

My goal for this course is for you to learn the computer science principles you need to be successful. The guidelines and rules are intended to facilitate your learning, and there is no substitute for putting in the time and effort to learn the material.

Life happens, though, and sometimes it will prevent you from focusing on CS. If something happens, please communicate with the professor as soon as possible. I don’t need details, but we do need to develop an alternative plan that will work for you and still ensure you will learn what you need to learn. We are partners in this academic adventure, and I want you to be successful.

Collaboration and Academic Honesty

Computer science, both academically and professionally, is a collaborative discipline. In any collaboration, however, all parties are expected to make their own contributions and to generously credit the contributions of others. In our class, therefore, collaboration on homework and programming assignments is encouraged, but you as an individual are responsible for understanding all the material in the assignment and doing your own work. Always strive to do your best, give generous credit to others, start early, and seek help early from both your professors and classmates.

The following rules are intended to help you get the most out of your education and to clarify the line between honest and dishonest work. The professor reserves the right to ask you to verbally explain the reasoning behind any answer or code that you turn in and to modify your project grade based on your answers. It is vitally important that you turn in work that is your own. Follow the guidelines for academic honesty.

If you have had a substantive discussion of any homework or programming solution with a classmate, then be sure to cite them in your report. If you are unsure of what constitutes "substantive", then ask us or err on the side of caution. You will not be penalized for working together. You must not copy answers or code from another student either by hand or electronically. Another way to think about it is that you should be talking English with one another, not Java.

The following rules apply to anything you hand in for a grade.

  • You may not copy anyone else's code under any circumstances. This includes online sources.
  • You may not permit any other student to see any part of your program.
  • You may not permit yourself to see any part of another student's program.
  • You may not publicly post your homework project code in a chat or discussion where another student can see or copy it.
  • You may consult online resources as part of your course work, but you may not copy code from online sources. If you get an idea of how to solve a problem from an online source, include a short citation at the top of your file.
